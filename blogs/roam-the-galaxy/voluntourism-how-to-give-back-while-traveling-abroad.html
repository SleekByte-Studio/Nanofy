Voluntourism: How to Give Back While Traveling Abroad

As travelers, we're incredibly fortunate to have the means and opportunity to explore different corners of the world. With this privilege comes a responsibility to protect these amazing destinations, support their local communities, and find meaningful ways to give back. While typical vacations allow you to experience the sights and culture of a place, incorporating volunteer work during your travels, also known as "voluntourism," lets you forge deeper connections, leave a positive impact, and bring home enriching experiences that money can't buy.

Through my own journeys around the globe, voluntourism has enriched my life in more ways than I can count. It has allowed me to move beyond the tourist bubble, become part of the fabric of communities I've visited, and work alongside inspiring people from all walks of life. More than just an ethical way to see the world, voluntourism introduces you to issues you might have been blind to otherwise while giving you a chance to directly address them. This hands-on approach brings about greater cross-cultural understanding, self-growth, and the power to spark meaningful change.

If you've been looking for ways to make your travels more purposeful, here are my top tips and insights for getting involved with voluntourism as you explore wonderful destinations around the world.

Do Your Research Not all volunteer opportunities are created equal. Seek out organizations, communities, and projects that have an established, respected track record for ethical, sustainable and impactful work. Avoid "volunteer" programs that feel more like exploitation or commodification, especially of children. Instead, find initiatives that allow you to work alongside local leaders and communities in a spirit of solidarity, not cultural voyeurism.

Online platforms like GoAbroad and Volunteer Forever are great resources for vetting programs, reading alumni reviews, and understanding the mission and methodologies behind each opportunity. You can filter volunteer work across causes like environmental conservation, teaching, women's empowerment, construction and more. I've had the most enriching experiences sticking to highly-vetted organizations recommended by trusted travel bloggers or non-profit authorities.

Put In The Effort Quality voluntourism experiences aren't a quick, superficial, check-box activity. They require you to roll up your sleeves and immerse yourself in the work, no matter how exhausting or uncomfortable it may get at times. I've sweated endlessly in the blazing jungle heat clearing miles of plastic waste from beaches across Southeast Asia. I've worked grueling hours on organic farm co-ops in New Zealand and Hawaii, constantly battling the relentless sun, dirt and fatigue. On preservation pursuits helping replant forests in South America, I've returned to my accommodations every evening completely caked in mud from head to toe.

While it can certainly be challenging, pushing past my limits and not shying away from the labor has allowed me to gain a greater connection to the people and places where I've worked. I've bonded with local communities over our shared struggles in the field and accomplishments at the end of long, rewarding days. Nothing brings people together like working side-by-side towards a common mission. My "vacations" have provided incredible strength, perspective and gratitude.

Stay Humble As visitors stepping into new communities, it's crucial that we check any sense of ego, saviorism or privilege mentality at the door. One of voluntourism's greatest lessons is that we are there to help, not take over or push Western ways onto other cultures and customs. Everything we're doing has been led and informed by generations of traditional ecological knowledge, ancestral wisdom and established community priorities.

We must be culturally respectful at all times, open to learning new approaches even if they push us outside our comfort zones. We're visitors in someone else's home, so humility is essential. Show up eager to learn, listen and serve however communities need - without judgment or assumption about the "right" way. On so many occasions, I've been in awe of the insight and creativity of local leaders overcoming challenges using their own ancestral knowledge and sustainable practices. We have so much to learn from people solving problems through their cultural ingenuity and human resilience.

Manage Expectations Unlike transactional forms of travel, voluntourism requires an abundant mindset of patience and managing expectations properly. We're guests invited into complex scenarios and any community's needs evolve rapidly based on changing environments or contexts. We have to let go of our own assumptions, time constraints or agendas in order to meaningfully adapt to the work at hand and what people need in that moment.

On a reforestation and education program in Costa Rica, I spent several intense weeks in a tiny community where deadly mudslides and flooding upended all plans and forced us to pivot to emergency response. It was devastating but also a profound lesson in showing up however I could to help in survival mode. In Guatemala, our initial education curriculums required complete overhaul based on fresh input from village elders to better align with cultural priorities. Those pivots ultimately led to more impactful outcomes, but forced us to constantly reset our own expectations along the way.

There have been so many humbling reminders that the work being done is not about us. Remaining flexible, patient and humble enough to follow the true needs of communities is everything.

Embrace Discomfort If you really want to push your personal growth and gain perspective-shifting realizations, you have to embrace some level of discomfort through voluntourism. On a construction project helping build smokeless chulah stoves for a remote village in India, I experienced first-hand just how physically draining and monotonous traditional labor can be. I didn't have the luxury of indoor plumbing, reliable electricity or my typical modern comforts. It was challenging and confronted me with my own privilege, but also underscored why sustainable infrastructure is so crucial for improving quality of life in these communities.

Voluntourism opportunities have also challenged me mentally and emotionally, beyond just the physical difficulties. In South Africa, I met young orphans and heard devastating first-hand stories from those directly impacted by HIV/AIDS, poverty and systemic injustice. Teaching girls in rural Kenya opened my eyes to the unimaginable barriers, sexism and lack of opportunity women face in pursuing their education and independence. These profound encounters have shattered aspects of my worldview, while also providing the greatest personal growth and appreciation for the basic dignities I easily take for granted at home.

Don't Quit After The Trip Finally, the most meaningful voluntourism experiences are ones that become integrated into your lifestyle and pursuits beyond just a single trip or project. I've developed lasting bonds with people and organizations around the world that I remain involved with supporting long after leaving - through fundraisers, skill-sharing, donations, advocacy campaigns and more.

The entire experience of getting to know a local culture, its challenges and priorities, has motivated me to keep showing up however I can from afar. I'm now an active supporter of micro-finance programs in Southeast Asia, a donor to NGOs combating environmental racism and pollution in Africa, and a remote mentor for young girls in Latin America aspiring to get into STEM fields. The more you invest in relationships and understanding of an issue, the more equipped you are to continue contributing in impactful ways.

At the end of the day, giving back through voluntourism has made me a more compassionate, open-minded and engaged global citizen. It has provided some of my richest travel memories filled with heart-changing encounters and eye-opening awakenings. More than just ensuring my travels are sustainable, getting hands-on with communities has deepened my connection to this awe-inspiring planet we all share.

Money comes and goes but the experiences you gain by contributing your time and effort create ripples that extend far beyond a single trip. If you have the chance, I urge you to find a voluntourism opportunity that resonates with you and take that chance to be the change you wish to see in the world. It might just transform you as profoundly as the projects you take part in.
